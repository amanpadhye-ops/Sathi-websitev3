Stories of Change

Every number in our impact data is a child with a name. These are some of them.

Children in Contact with Railways
Alok Kannojia
12 years old · Rescued March 2023 · Pune
"SATHI planted the seed of progress. We just nurtured it." — Alok's mother

On 30th March 2023, 12-year-old Alok and his 13-year-old friend Atharv were spotted at Pune Railway Station heading to Delhi, alone and without a plan. SATHI's outreach team brought them in.

At the shelter, Alok was frightened and lashing out. Over counselling, the story emerged: Atharv had secretly spent ₹40,000 from his mother's account and, fearing discovery, had convinced Alok to run away with him. Alok stayed at the shelter for 17 days. In that time, something shifted.

Follow-Up Visit — May 2024

A year later, SATHI visited Alok at his new home. His family had moved to distance him from old influences. Alok helps his father at the laundry after studying, never leaves without telling his parents. 'SATHI planted the seed,' his mother said. 'We just nurtured it.'

Aryan Gavli
15 years old at rescue · Now earning ₹25,000/month as JCB Driver
"If SATHI hadn't saved me, my life might have been very different."

Aryan was 15 when SATHI found him at Pune station on 31st January 2019, alone and scared, heading to his maternal uncle's home without knowing the way. He had run away after his father scolded him for bunking school.

At the shelter, Aryan opened up slowly. SATHI told his father directly: expectations are fair, but the approach needed to change. His father listened.

Follow-Up Visit — May 2024, Five Years Later

SATHI met Aryan at his workplace. He earns ₹25,000 a month as a JCB driver with the Pune Municipal Corporation. He lives with his parents and asked if he could visit the shelter to speak to the boys there.

Balram & Jairam Singh
16 & 13 years old · Rescued November 2018
"Balram is studying Mechanical Engineering. Jairam wants to follow. Their chacha funded all of it."

Two brothers, orphaned by their father's death, living with their chacha. After being scolded for not studying, both decided to go back to their mother in Bihar. SATHI's counsellors helped them see their uncle's scolding came from investment, not cruelty. The chacha acknowledged his mistake. The boys went home.

Follow-Up Visit — May 2024, Six Years Later

Balram, now 21, is studying Mechanical Engineering at Nutan Maratha Engineering College. Jairam, now 18, has completed Class 12 and wants to follow his brother. Their chacha funded it all. 'Since 2018, nothing like that has happened again. They talk to me now.'

Deepak Londe
14 years old at rescue · Now earning ₹15,000/month
"One day at SATHI brought about a profound change. Today, he is responsible." — Deepak's parents

Deepak had run away before. Twice. On 10th March 2019, after being beaten by older boys at school, he walked out and spent two days on the streets before SATHI found him.

The counsellor told him something he hadn't thought about: running was the easy way out. Fighting through adversity was what prepared you for life. Deepak heard it.

Follow-Up Visit — May 2024, Five Years Later

Deepak, now 19, completed a one-year ITI course and found a job at Seinumero, an automotive parts manufacturer. He earns ₹15,000 a month. His family has a shared dream of owning a home, and they are saving for it.

Mahesh
16 years old at rescue · Now driving commercially
"Academics was never my passion. But I always loved driving. Now I do it for a living."

Mahesh wasn't running from anything. On 3rd January 2019, the 16-year-old simply decided to go to Pune station with a friend and find a job. He didn't find one. SATHI found him.

People of SATHI — Field Team
The Night Before the Board Exam
Rajveer Sir · Railway Outreach Worker
"The boy's board exam was the next morning. And there he was, alone on a platform in a city that wasn't his."

The boy was from Nanded. He was 15, and his Class 10 board exams began the following morning. He had run away after a fight with his father and ended up at Pune station.

Rajveer sir spotted him in the evening, sitting still. He sat down and started talking. Slowly the story came out: a fight at home, a train taken without thinking, an exam at 8 AM.

SATHI worked through the night to coordinate with his school in Nanded. His parents arrived early. The team arranged for him to appear at a centre closer to Pune. He sat his exam that day. He passed.

Seven Months. One Video Call.
Govind Sir · Railway & Shelter Outreach
"We almost gave up," Govind sir said. "We didn't. That's the only reason it worked."

The boy couldn't speak clearly. He had a cognitive disability — names, places, directions all came out fractured. He didn't know his city, his parents' names, his village or school. What SATHI had was a child, a shelter, and the will to keep trying.

For seven months they worked the case. Then someone called schools across the region asking if any principal recognised a missing child with a disability. One did.

Seven months after the boy arrived, a video call was arranged. His parents appeared on screen. The moment they saw their son, they broke down. His mother couldn't speak. His father kept saying his name.

The Boy from Ahmednagar
Lata Ma'am · Shelter & Restoration Counsellor
"His mother died six months before he could get home. We couldn't bring her back. But we could bring him home. That had to be enough."

The boy was from Ahmednagar. He came to the shelter alone, disoriented, not ready to talk. Counselling began slowly.

Gradually the story emerged: his mother had HIV and had been admitted to hospital. The boy had ended up far from home. Family tracing began. Relatives were found. The process took time.

His mother was in hospital for six months. SATHI kept working the case. His mother died before he could get home. The boy was eventually restored to his relatives in the village.

Five Years to Find Alibaug
Saroj Sir · Family Tracing Specialist
"That boy's father made keys for a living. We just had to find the right lock."

The boy had arrived in Delhi. He said he was from near 'Jama Masjid.' The problem: every town with a significant Muslim population has one. As a locator, it was nearly useless.

SATHI made four home visit trips over five years. Four times the pieces didn't quite fit. Then one detail broke the case open: the boy said his father worked with metal. Alibaug district in Raigad is known for locksmiths.

They found the home on that trip. The father was a locksmith. The family had never stopped looking.
